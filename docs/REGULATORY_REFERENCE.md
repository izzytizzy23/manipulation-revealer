# Regulatory Reference — Persuasion Shield

**Disclaimer:** This document is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Consult qualified legal counsel for compliance decisions. Regulatory references are based on publicly available texts as of April 2026 and may not reflect subsequent amendments.

---

## Overview

Persuasion Shield maps detected tactics to relevant regulatory frameworks across four jurisdictions: the European Union, the United States, Canada, and Australia. This helps users and compliance teams identify content that may cross legal boundaries.

---

## European Union

### 1. EU AI Act (2024)

- **Key Provision:** Article 5 classifies AI systems that use subliminal techniques to materially distort behaviour as "unacceptable risk" — these are **prohibited**.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Fear appeal, emotional manipulation, false dilemma, scarcity, urgency
- **Why It Matters:** If AI-driven content recommendation optimises for tactics that bypass conscious awareness (e.g., fear-based engagement), this may constitute prohibited subliminal manipulation. Article 5(1)(a) specifically targets techniques that deploy subliminal components beyond a person's consciousness.
- **Penalties:** Up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

### 2. EU Digital Services Act (DSA)

- **Key Provision:** Requires very large online platforms (VLOPs) to assess and mitigate systemic risks, including manipulation of public discourse. Mandates transparency in algorithmic recommendation.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Social proof, bandwagon, authority
- **Why It Matters:** Algorithmically amplified social proof and bandwagon effects are systemic risks that platforms must assess, audit, and report on under Articles 34-35.
- **Penalties:** Up to 6% of global annual turnover.

### 3. GDPR (Articles 5, 6, 22)

- **Key Provision:** Requires lawful basis for personal data processing. Article 22 gives individuals the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof, bandwagon
- **Why It Matters:** When persuasive content is algorithmically targeted based on user profiling (identity, behavioural data), the combination of personalisation + persuasion tactics may trigger Article 22 protections and require explicit consent under Article 6.
- **Penalties:** Up to EUR 20 million or 4% of global annual turnover.

### 4. EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC)

- **Key Provision:** Prohibits misleading actions (Article 6), misleading omissions (Article 7), and aggressive commercial practices (Articles 8-9). Annex I contains a blacklist of practices that are always unfair.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Scarcity, urgency, anchoring, authority, social proof, false dilemma
- **Why It Matters:** Fabricated scarcity, false urgency, inflated original prices, fake expert endorsements, and manufactured social proof are all potentially prohibited as misleading or aggressive commercial practices.
- **Penalties:** Varies by member state transposition; EU recommends effective, proportionate, dissuasive penalties.

## United States

### 5. FTC Act Section 5

- **Key Provision:** Prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce." The FTC has actively pursued enforcement against "dark patterns."
- **Relevant Tactics:** Scarcity, urgency, anchoring, false dilemma, authority
- **Why It Matters:** Fabricated scarcity, fake countdown timers, inflated original prices, and unsubstantiated expert claims are within the FTC's enforcement scope. The FTC's 2022 enforcement policy statement specifically targets dark patterns.
- **Notable Enforcement:** FTC v. Age of Empires (dark patterns), FTC v. Fortnite (manipulative design).

### 6. FTC Endorsement Guides (16 CFR Part 255)

- **Key Provision:** Requires truthful endorsements and disclosure of material connections. Updated in 2023 to cover social media influencers, fake reviews, and AI-generated endorsements.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Authority, social proof, identity
- **Why It Matters:** Fake expert claims, fabricated testimonials, undisclosed paid endorsements, and misleading "as seen on" claims violate these guidelines.
- **Penalties:** Up to $50,120 per violation (adjusted annually).

### 7. CCPA / CPRA (California)

- **Key Provision:** Grants consumers rights over personal information including the right to know, delete, opt-out of sale, and limit use of sensitive information. CPRA (effective 2023) adds automated decision-making protections.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof
- **Why It Matters:** When persuasion tactics are personalised using consumer data (identity-based targeting, behavioural profiling), CCPA/CPRA rights are triggered.

### 8. COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)

- **Key Provision:** Requires verifiable parental consent before collecting data from children under 13. Prohibits unfair practices targeting children.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Fear appeal, emotional manipulation, bandwagon, identity
- **Why It Matters:** Manipulative tactics targeting minors face heightened scrutiny. The FTC has proposed expanding COPPA to restrict targeted advertising to children.

### 9. State Consumer Protection Laws

- Multiple states have consumer protection statutes (e.g., New York GBL § 349, Texas DTPA, Illinois CFDBPA) that prohibit deceptive trade practices, covering many of the same tactics flagged by this extension.

## Canada

### 10. Competition Act (Sections 52, 74)

- **Key Provision:** Section 52 (criminal): Prohibits knowingly or recklessly making false or misleading representations to the public. Section 74 (civil): Prohibits representations that are false or misleading in a material respect.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Scarcity, urgency, anchoring, authority, social proof, false dilemma
- **Why It Matters:** Fabricated scarcity claims, misleading price comparisons (anchoring), false authority endorsements, and manufactured social proof are all potentially actionable. The Competition Bureau actively investigates digital advertising practices.
- **Penalties:** Criminal (Section 52): Up to CAD 200,000 fine and/or 1 year imprisonment (summary), or up to 14 years imprisonment (indictable). Civil (Section 74): Up to CAD 10 million (first offence) or CAD 15 million (subsequent).

### 11. PIPEDA / CPPA

- **Key Provision:** PIPEDA governs collection, use, and disclosure of personal information in commercial activities using 10 fair information principles. The proposed Consumer Privacy Protection Act (CPPA, Bill C-27) would replace PIPEDA with stronger enforcement.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof, bandwagon
- **Why It Matters:** When persuasion is driven by personal data profiling (identity-based targeting, behavioural analytics), PIPEDA's consent and purpose limitation principles apply.
- **Penalties:** PIPEDA: up to CAD 100,000 (federal court). CPPA (proposed): up to 5% of global revenue or CAD 25 million.

### 12. CASL (Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation)

- **Key Provision:** Prohibits sending commercial electronic messages (CEMs) without express or implied consent. Requires clear identification of sender and functioning unsubscribe mechanism. Prohibits false or misleading representations in CEMs.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Urgency, scarcity, fear appeal, authority
- **Why It Matters:** Deceptive subject lines, false sender claims, and manipulative urgency/scarcity in commercial electronic messages violate CASL. Applies to messages sent from or received in Canada.
- **Penalties:** Up to CAD 1 million (individuals) or CAD 10 million (organisations) per violation.

### 13. Provincial Consumer Protection Acts

- **Key Provision:** Each province has consumer protection legislation (e.g., Ontario Consumer Protection Act 2002, Quebec Consumer Protection Act, BC Business Practices and Consumer Protection Act) prohibiting unfair practices, false representations, and unconscionable conduct.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Scarcity, urgency, anchoring, false dilemma, fear appeal, authority
- **Why It Matters:** Provincial legislation often provides broader consumer protections than federal law and allows direct private action by consumers.

## Australia

### 14. Australian Consumer Law (ACL) — Schedule 2, CCA 2010

- **Key Provision:** Section 18: Prohibits conduct that is misleading or deceptive (or likely to mislead or deceive). Sections 20-22: Prohibit unconscionable conduct. Sections 29-37: Prohibit specific false representations.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Scarcity, urgency, anchoring, authority, social proof, false dilemma, fear appeal
- **Why It Matters:** The ACL has the broadest reach of any Australian consumer regulation. Fabricated scarcity, misleading price representations, false authority claims, and fear-based pressure tactics can all constitute misleading or deceptive conduct or unconscionable conduct. The ACCC actively monitors digital platforms.
- **Penalties:** Up to AUD 50 million (corporations), AUD 2.5 million (individuals), or 3x the benefit obtained, whichever is greater.

### 15. Australian Online Safety Act 2021

- **Key Provision:** Establishes the eSafety Commissioner with powers to investigate and enforce removal of harmful online content. Covers cyber-abuse, image-based abuse, and harmful online content including deceptive design patterns.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Fear appeal, emotional manipulation, false dilemma
- **Why It Matters:** Content that uses fear and emotional manipulation to cause harm falls within the Act's scope, particularly when targeting vulnerable users (children, elderly).

### 16. Australian Privacy Act 1988 (APPs)

- **Key Provision:** 13 Australian Privacy Principles governing collection, use, disclosure, quality, security, access, and correction of personal information.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof
- **Why It Matters:** When persuasion is driven by personal data profiling or behavioural targeting, the Privacy Act's consent and collection limitation principles apply. The 2024 reforms strengthened individual rights and penalties.
- **Penalties:** Up to AUD 50 million, 3x the benefit obtained, or 30% of adjusted turnover (whichever is greatest).

### 17. Consumer Data Right (CDR)

- **Key Provision:** Establishes data transparency and portability obligations, currently in banking, energy, and telecommunications. Consumers can authorise sharing of their data and revoke access.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof
- **Why It Matters:** In CDR-regulated sectors (banking, energy, telco), persuasive content driven by consumer data must comply with CDR rules around consent, data minimisation, and transparency.

## Regulatory Flag Levels

| Level | Criteria | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Standard persuasion, single tactic, neutral context | "Join thousands of happy customers" |
| **Medium** | Multiple tactics OR sensitive context (financial/health) | "Experts say you should act now — limited availability" |
| **High** | 3+ tactics AND/OR financial promises, health claims without evidence, manipulative urgency in regulated context | "Guaranteed 300% returns — offer expires in 1 hour. Don't be left behind." |
