# Regulatory Reference — Persuasion Shield

## Overview

Persuasion Shield maps detected tactics to relevant regulatory frameworks, helping users and compliance teams identify content that may cross legal boundaries.

## Regulatory Frameworks

### 1. EU AI Act (2024)

- **Jurisdiction:** European Union
- **Key Provision:** Article 5 classifies AI systems that use subliminal techniques to materially distort behaviour as "unacceptable risk" — these are prohibited.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Fear appeal, emotional manipulation, false dilemma, scarcity, urgency
- **Why It Matters:** If AI-driven content recommendation optimises for tactics that bypass conscious awareness (e.g., fear-based engagement), this may constitute prohibited subliminal manipulation.

### 2. EU Digital Services Act (DSA)

- **Jurisdiction:** European Union
- **Key Provision:** Requires very large online platforms (VLOPs) to assess and mitigate systemic risks, including manipulation of public discourse. Mandates transparency in algorithmic recommendation systems.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Social proof, bandwagon, authority
- **Why It Matters:** Algorithmically amplified social proof and bandwagon effects are systemic risks that platforms must assess and report on.

### 3. US FTC Act Section 5

- **Jurisdiction:** United States
- **Key Provision:** Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in commerce. The FTC has actively pursued enforcement against "dark patterns."
- **Relevant Tactics:** Scarcity, urgency, anchoring, false dilemma, authority
- **Why It Matters:** Fabricated scarcity, fake countdown timers, inflated original prices, and unsubstantiated expert claims are all within the FTC's enforcement scope.

### 4. GDPR Article 22

- **Jurisdiction:** European Union
- **Key Provision:** Right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produces legal or similarly significant effects.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof, bandwagon
- **Why It Matters:** When persuasive content is algorithmically targeted based on user profiling, the combination of personalisation + persuasion tactics may trigger Article 22 protections.

### 5. Australian Online Safety Act

- **Jurisdiction:** Australia
- **Key Provision:** Covers deceptive design patterns and harmful online content. The eSafety Commissioner has broad powers to investigate and enforce.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Fear appeal, emotional manipulation, false dilemma
- **Why It Matters:** Content that uses fear and emotional manipulation to cause harm falls within the Act's scope, particularly when targeting vulnerable users.

### 6. Consumer Data Right (CDR)

- **Jurisdiction:** Australia
- **Key Provision:** Establishes data transparency obligations for how consumer behavioural data is collected, used, and shared.
- **Relevant Tactics:** Identity, social proof
- **Why It Matters:** When user behavioural data drives persuasive targeting (showing identity-relevant or socially-primed content), CDR transparency obligations may apply.

## Regulatory Flag Levels

| Level | Criteria | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Standard persuasion, single tactic, neutral context | "Join thousands of happy customers" |
| **Medium** | Multiple tactics OR sensitive context (financial/health) | "Experts say you should act now — limited availability" |
| **High** | 3+ tactics AND/OR financial promises, health claims without evidence, manipulative urgency in regulated context | "Guaranteed 300% returns — offer expires in 1 hour. Don't be left behind." |
